Two planes through a line, at a given angle

A worked example — the answer first, then every idea you need, then the full derivation, then how to check it.

The Problem

Find equations for the two planes that intersect the plane \( \textcolor{#5A6B85}{x - y = 0} \) at an angle of \( \tfrac{\pi}{3} \) and have line of intersection given by \( \textcolor{#B8860B}{\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle 0,0,2\rangle + t\langle 1,1,1\rangle}. \)

The Answer

$$ \textcolor{#2D7D6F}{x - z + 2 = 0} \qquad\text{and}\qquad \textcolor{#C2562B}{y - z + 2 = 0} $$

Equivalently: \( \textcolor{#2D7D6F}{z = x + 2} \) and \( \textcolor{#C2562B}{z = y + 2} \).

Throughout this lesson, color is consistent: gold is the given line L, slate is the fixed plane x − y = 0, and teal / orange are the two planes we're hunting for.

Words You Need First

Five ideas, briefly

Everything in this problem is built from five small pieces. If these are solid, the solution is short.

Normal vector

A vector \( \mathbf{n} \) that sticks straight out of a plane — perpendicular to every direction lying in the plane. It is the plane's "orientation arrow."

Read it off the equation for free: the plane \( ax+by+cz=d \) has normal \( \mathbf{n}=\langle a,b,c\rangle \). So \( \textcolor{#5A6B85}{x-y=0} \) has normal \( \textcolor{#5A6B85}{\mathbf{n}_0=\langle 1,-1,0\rangle} \).

Dot product & angle

For any vectors, \( \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta \), so

$$ \cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|}. $$

Special case to memorize: \( \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=0 \iff \mathbf{u}\perp\mathbf{v} \).

Point–normal form of a plane

A plane is completely determined by one point \( (x_0,y_0,z_0) \) on it and one normal vector \( \langle a,b,c\rangle \):

$$ a(x-x_0)+b(y-y_0)+c(z-z_0)=0. $$

This is the engine of the whole solution: find the point (given), find the normal (the real work).

Vector form of a line

\( \textcolor{#B8860B}{\mathbf{r}(t)=\langle 0,0,2\rangle + t\langle 1,1,1\rangle} \) means: start at the point \( P_0=(0,0,2) \), then walk in the direction \( \mathbf{d}=\langle 1,1,1\rangle \). Every value of \( t \) gives one point of the line: \( (t,\,t,\,t+2) \).

Angle between two planes

Defined as the (acute) angle between their normal vectors. Since each normal could point to either side of its plane, we take an absolute value to force the acute answer:

$$ \cos\theta = \frac{|\mathbf{n}_1\cdot\mathbf{n}_2|}{\|\mathbf{n}_1\|\,\|\mathbf{n}_2\|}. $$

Why does this work? Rotate each plane's normal by 90° and it lies flat in its plane — rotating both vectors by the same amount doesn't change the angle between them. So the angle the normals make equals the dihedral angle where the planes meet.

See It Before You Solve It

The geometry of the problem

Draw the cast of characters before touching algebra. The line L passes through \( P_0=(0,0,2) \) with direction \( \mathbf{d}=\langle 1,1,1\rangle \). The fixed plane is \( x-y=0 \): all points whose first two coordinates are equal — a vertical sheet standing over the diagonal \( y=x \) of the floor.

A quiet but important observation The line L actually lies inside the fixed plane. Check it: the point \( (0,0,2) \) satisfies \( x-y=0-0=0 \) ✓, and the direction satisfies \( \mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{n}_0 = \langle1,1,1\rangle\cdot\langle1,-1,0\rangle = 1-1+0 = 0 \) ✓, so moving along L never leaves the plane. Every point of L has the form \( (t,t,t+2) \) — first two coordinates equal, as required. The two planes we seek are hinged on a line drawn inside the slate plane itself, tilted up out of it by 60°.

Now the key mental picture. Imagine every possible plane that contains L. They form a family of pages hinged on a single spine — the spine is L. As a page rotates about the spine, the angle it makes with the fixed slate plane sweeps continuously from 0° up to 90° and back. Somewhere in that rotation it passes through exactly 60° — once tilting one way, once tilting the other. That is why the problem promises two planes.

So the strategy writes itself:

Strategy A plane = a point + a normal. We already have the point, \( P_0=(0,0,2) \). Give the unknown normal a name, \( \mathbf{n}=\langle a,b,c\rangle \), then make it satisfy two demands: (1) the plane must contain the line, and (2) the plane must meet \( x-y=0 \) at \( \pi/3 \). Two demands, and the answer pops out.
The Derivation

Step by step

Step 1 — Contain the line

If a plane contains L, then the direction of L, \( \mathbf{d}=\langle1,1,1\rangle \), is a direction lying inside the plane. And the normal is perpendicular to everything inside the plane (Fig. 1). So:

$$ \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{d}=0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; a+b+c=0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \boxed{\,c=-a-b\,} $$

Why this matters: it kills one unknown. We started with three unknowns \( a,b,c \); now only \( a \) and \( b \) are free. Geometrically, this single equation is the algebraic name for the whole "hinged family of pages" in Fig. 3.

Step 2 — Pass through the point

The plane must contain \( P_0=(0,0,2) \) (any point of L works; this one is handed to us). Point–normal form gives

$$ a(x-0)+b(y-0)+c(z-2)=0, $$

and substituting \( c=-a-b \), the general plane containing the line is

$$ ax+by-(a+b)(z-2)=0. $$

Note that this step adds no new constraint on \( a,b \) — it just records where the plane sits. The point pins the plane in space; the normal still controls its tilt.

Step 3 — Impose the angle

The fixed plane \( \textcolor{#5A6B85}{x-y=0} \) has normal \( \textcolor{#5A6B85}{\mathbf{n}_0=\langle1,-1,0\rangle} \) (read straight off its coefficients). The angle-between-planes formula with \( \theta=\pi/3 \), \( \cos\frac{\pi}{3}=\frac12 \):

$$ \frac{|\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{n}_0|}{\|\mathbf{n}\|\,\|\mathbf{n}_0\|}=\frac12. $$

Compute each piece, using \( c=-a-b \) from Step 1:

$$ \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{n}_0 = a(1)+b(-1)+c(0) = a-b, \qquad \|\mathbf{n}_0\|=\sqrt{1^2+(-1)^2+0^2}=\sqrt{2}, $$ $$ \|\mathbf{n}\|^2 = a^2+b^2+(a+b)^2 = a^2+b^2+a^2+2ab+b^2 = 2\,(a^2+ab+b^2). $$

Substitute everything into the formula:

$$ \frac{|a-b|}{\sqrt{2(a^2+ab+b^2)}\cdot\sqrt{2}} = \frac12 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \frac{|a-b|}{2\sqrt{a^2+ab+b^2}} = \frac12 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; |a-b| = \sqrt{a^2+ab+b^2}. $$

Both sides are nonnegative, so squaring is safe — no fake solutions can sneak in:

$$ (a-b)^2 = a^2+ab+b^2 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; a^2-2ab+b^2 = a^2+ab+b^2 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; -2ab = ab \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \boxed{\,ab=0\,} $$

Pause and appreciate this. A messy square-root equation collapsed to two words: \( a=0 \) or \( b=0 \). Whenever a problem promises "the two planes," expect the algebra to hand you exactly two cases like this.

Step 4 — The two cases

Case \( b=0 \): then \( c=-a \), so \( \mathbf{n}=\langle a,0,-a\rangle = a\,\langle1,0,-1\rangle \). Any nonzero scalar multiple of a normal describes the same plane, so choose the clean one, \( \textcolor{#2D7D6F}{\mathbf{n}_1=\langle1,0,-1\rangle} \). Point–normal form through \( (0,0,2) \):

$$ 1(x-0)+0(y-0)-1(z-2)=0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \textcolor{#2D7D6F}{x-z+2=0}. $$

Case \( a=0 \): then \( c=-b \), giving \( \textcolor{#C2562B}{\mathbf{n}_2=\langle0,1,-1\rangle} \), and

$$ 0(x-0)+1(y-0)-1(z-2)=0 \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \textcolor{#C2562B}{y-z+2=0}. $$

(If \( a=0 \) and \( b=0 \), then \( c=0 \) too and \( \mathbf{n}=\mathbf{0} \) — not a normal vector at all. So the two cases are genuinely just two.)

Why Exactly Two?

Look straight down the hinge

Here is the cleanest way to see the whole problem at once. Stand so the line L points directly at your eye. Every plane that contains L now looks like a straight line through a single point (you're seeing each plane edge-on, and L itself collapses to a dot). The fixed plane contains L too, so it's one of those lines. Angles between planes appear as honest 2-D angles in this view.

This picture also explains the count in advance: for any target angle strictly between 0° and 90° there are exactly two planes; at exactly 90° the two merge into one; at 0° the only "plane" is the fixed plane itself.

Where Students Lose Points

Four classic pitfalls

1
Dropping the absolute value. Normals are double-ended: \( \langle0,1,-1\rangle \) and \( \langle0,-1,1\rangle \) describe the same plane but their dot products with \( \mathbf{n}_0 \) have opposite signs. Without \( |\cdot| \) you can get \( \cos\theta=-\tfrac12 \) and wrongly discard a correct plane (notice \( \mathbf{n}_2\cdot\mathbf{n}_0=-1 \) above — the orange plane only survives because of the absolute value).
2
Forgetting that the line must be in the plane. The condition \( \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{d}=0 \) alone gives a plane parallel to the line. You also need a point of the line on the plane — that's Step 2. Skip it and your plane floats off to the side.
3
Worrying about "infinitely many normals." Students see free parameters \( a,b \) and panic. But normals scale: \( \langle 5,0,-5\rangle \) and \( \langle1,0,-1\rangle \) give equations \( 5x-5z+10=0 \) and \( x-z+2=0 \) — the same plane. Pick the simplest representative and move on.
4
Expecting one answer (or four). The end-on picture (Fig. 4) settles the count before you compute: a page hinged on L hits a given acute angle exactly twice per rotation, once on each side of the fixed plane. If your algebra produces one or three solutions, hunt for the mistake.
Now You

A practice problem with the same skeleton

Find equations for the two planes that intersect the plane \( y - z = 0 \) at an angle of \( \tfrac{\pi}{3} \) and have line of intersection \( \mathbf{r}(t) = t\langle 1,1,1\rangle \) (the line through the origin in direction \( \langle1,1,1\rangle \)).

Before computing, predict: where does the line sit relative to the plane \( y-z=0 \)? How many answers should you expect, and why? Then run the five steps.

Reveal the worked answer

Setup. Point \( P_0=(0,0,0) \), direction \( \mathbf{d}=\langle1,1,1\rangle \), fixed normal \( \mathbf{n}_0=\langle0,1,-1\rangle \). (Check: the line lies in the fixed plane, since \( 0-0=0 \) and \( \mathbf{d}\cdot\mathbf{n}_0=0+1-1=0 \).)

Contain the line. \( \mathbf{n}=\langle a,b,c\rangle \) with \( a+b+c=0 \), so \( c=-a-b \).

Angle. \( \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{n}_0 = b-c = b-(-a-b) = a+2b \), and \( \|\mathbf{n}\|^2 = 2(a^2+ab+b^2) \) exactly as before. So

$$ \frac{|a+2b|}{2\sqrt{a^2+ab+b^2}}=\frac12 \;\Longrightarrow\; (a+2b)^2 = a^2+ab+b^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; 3ab+3b^2=0 \;\Longrightarrow\; b(a+b)=0. $$

Case \( b=0 \): \( \mathbf{n}=\langle1,0,-1\rangle \), through the origin: \( \;x-z=0 \).

Case \( a=-b \): take \( b=-1 \), so \( \mathbf{n}=\langle1,-1,0\rangle \), through the origin: \( \;x-y=0 \).

Answer: \( x-z=0 \) and \( x-y=0 \). Quick check on the second: \( |\langle1,-1,0\rangle\cdot\langle0,1,-1\rangle| / (\sqrt2\,\sqrt2) = |-1|/2 = \tfrac12 \) ✓.

Notice the case condition came out as \( b(a+b)=0 \) instead of \( ab=0 \) — the exact factorization depends on the fixed plane, but the structure (two clean cases) is the same.

The Whole Method on a Card
Recipe: plane(s) through a given line at a given angle to a given plane 1. Name the unknown normal \( \mathbf{n}=\langle a,b,c\rangle \).  2. Contain the line: \( \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{d}=0 \) (eliminates one variable).  3. Write the plane through a point of the line in point–normal form.  4. Impose \( \cos\theta = |\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{n}_0| \,/\, (\|\mathbf{n}\|\|\mathbf{n}_0\|) \), square, and factor — expect two cases.  5. Pick the simplest normal in each case, write both planes, and verify the line and both angles.
